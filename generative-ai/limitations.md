# Generative AI: Limitations for Oncology Clinical Trials with Physical AI

## Overview

Generative AI's power to create novel content introduces risks that are qualitatively
different from those of discriminative models. In oncology — where errors can cause patient
harm and regulatory compliance is mandatory — the limitations of generative AI demand
rigorous engineering controls. This document addresses hallucination risk, validation burden,
regulatory uncertainty, and computational requirements as they apply to federated oncology
settings with Physical AI integration.

## 1. Hallucination Risk in Clinical Contexts

Generative models produce plausible but factually incorrect outputs, and in oncology this
failure mode is dangerous.

- **Clinical hallucination taxonomy**: LLM hallucinations in oncology include fabricated
  drug names, invented clinical trial results, incorrect drug-gene interactions, wrong
  dosing recommendations, and spurious citations to nonexistent papers. Studies of LLMs
  answering oncology questions show factual error rates of 5–15% even in state-of-the-art
  models, with errors often presented with high confidence.
- **Synthetic data hallucinations**: Generative models producing synthetic imaging or tabular
  data may generate anatomically impossible structures, physiologically implausible lab value
  combinations, or statistically inconsistent covariate patterns. If these synthetic artifacts
  enter training pipelines, they corrupt downstream model performance — a phenomenon termed
  "model collapse" or "data poisoning by synthesis."
- **Hallucination detection is unsolved**: Current hallucination detection methods (self-
  consistency checking, retrieval verification, confidence calibration) reduce but do not
  eliminate hallucination. In clinical contexts where even rare hallucinations can cause
  harm, residual hallucination rates of 1–3% after mitigation may be unacceptable.
- **Compounding errors in agentic workflows**: When generative agents execute multi-step
  clinical workflows, errors in early steps propagate and amplify through subsequent steps.
  A hallucinated eligibility criterion in trial matching can exclude suitable patients or
  include ineligible ones — both with serious consequences.
- **Reference**: Omiye et al., "Large Language Models Propagate Race-Based Medicine,"
  npj Digital Medicine, 2023; Singhal et al., "Large language models encode clinical
  knowledge," Nature, 2023.

## 2. Validation Burden

The open-ended nature of generative outputs makes validation fundamentally harder than for
discriminative models.

- **Output space is unbounded**: A supervised classifier outputs one of N classes; a
  generative model outputs free-form text, images, or structured data. Validating every
  possible output is impossible, requiring statistical validation frameworks that bound error
  rates across the output distribution rather than exhaustively testing individual outputs.
- **Synthetic data validation pipeline**: Every synthetic dataset must be validated for:
  (a) statistical fidelity — distributional similarity to real data, (b) privacy — ensuring
  no memorized patient data is reproduced, (c) utility — demonstrated improvement in
  downstream task performance, and (d) fairness — no amplification of demographic biases.
  This four-dimensional validation is expensive and must be repeated whenever the generative
  model is updated.
- **Clinical validation of LLM outputs**: Treatment recommendations generated by LLM
  copilots require expert review before clinical use. The time required for expert validation
  may negate the efficiency gains the LLM provides, particularly for complex multi-modal
  cases where the LLM's reasoning chain must be verified step by step.
- **Temporal validation decay**: Generative models trained on data up to a cutoff date
  produce outputs that become increasingly outdated as treatment guidelines evolve. Unlike
  supervised models that can be evaluated on fixed test sets, generative model validity
  degrades continuously and requires ongoing validation against current evidence.
- **Adversarial robustness**: Generative models are susceptible to prompt injection, jailbreaking, and adversarial inputs that cause them to produce harmful outputs. In clinical settings, this creates a security surface that must be continuously monitored.

## 3. Regulatory Uncertainty

The regulatory framework for generative AI in healthcare is nascent and rapidly evolving,
creating compliance risk for clinical deployment.

- **No established regulatory pathway**: FDA has approved dozens of supervised learning SaMD
  (Software as a Medical Device) products through 510(k) and De Novo pathways, establishing
  clear precedent. No generative AI system has received FDA clearance for autonomous clinical
  decision-making. The regulatory expectations for validating generative outputs are undefined.
- **Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) challenges**: FDA's PCCP framework allows
  pre-specified model updates, but generative models — especially LLMs with billions of
  parameters retrained on new data — undergo changes that are difficult to characterize
  within a predetermined change protocol.
- **Synthetic data regulatory status**: Whether synthetic data can be used to validate
  medical devices (as supplementary training data or as virtual clinical trial evidence)
  is an open regulatory question. FDA has published discussion papers but no binding guidance.
- **Liability for generated content**: When an LLM copilot generates a treatment
  recommendation that leads to adverse outcomes, liability allocation between the model
  developer, the deploying institution, and the treating clinician is legally untested.
- **International regulatory divergence**: EU AI Act classifies medical AI as high-risk with
  specific requirements for transparency and human oversight. These requirements interact
  unpredictably with generative AI capabilities, and compliance strategies that work under
  FDA may not satisfy EU AI Act requirements.
- **Reference**: FDA, "Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning (AI/ML)-Enabled Medical
  Devices," 2023; EU AI Act, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689.

## 4. Computational Requirements

Generative AI, particularly LLMs and diffusion models, demands computational resources that
challenge clinical infrastructure and federated deployment.

- **LLM inference cost**: A 70B-parameter LLM requires 4x A100 80GB GPUs for FP16 inference
  and generates 30–50 tokens/second. For real-time clinical copilot applications requiring
  sub-second response, smaller models (7B–13B) may be necessary, but these sacrifice
  accuracy on complex oncology reasoning.
- **Diffusion model training cost**: Training a medical imaging diffusion model to generate
  high-quality 3D CT volumes requires 1,000–5,000 A100 GPU-hours per training run. In
  federated settings, this cost is multiplied across sites.
- **Memory requirements for fine-tuning**: Full fine-tuning of LLMs is prohibitively
  expensive for most clinical sites. Parameter-efficient methods (LoRA, QLoRA, adapter
  tuning) reduce memory requirements but introduce additional hyperparameters and may
  sacrifice fine-tuning quality.
- **Edge deployment limitations**: Physical AI systems operating on edge hardware (NVIDIA
  Jetson, embedded GPUs) have insufficient memory and compute for running large generative
  models locally. This forces cloud inference with associated latency, connectivity
  requirements, and data privacy concerns.
- **Inference cost scaling**: Unlike discriminative models with fixed inference cost,
  generative model cost scales with output length. A treatment planning copilot producing
  a detailed analysis may generate thousands of tokens, incurring 10–100x the inference
  cost of a single classification prediction.

## 5. Bias Amplification and Fairness

- **Training data bias reproduction**: Generative models trained on historical clinical data
  reproduce and can amplify existing biases — racial disparities in treatment, gender biases
  in symptom assessment, socioeconomic biases in trial enrollment. LLMs have been shown to
  propagate race-based medicine concepts present in their training corpora.
- **Synthetic data fairness**: Generative models trained on imbalanced datasets produce
  synthetic data that reflects the same imbalances. Without explicit fairness constraints,
  synthetic data augmentation can worsen rather than improve demographic parity.
- **Differential performance across languages**: LLM-based clinical tools perform better
  in English than in other languages, creating disparities in multi-national federated
  oncology trials where clinical documentation may be in local languages.

## 6. Integration Challenges in Federated Settings

- **Model versioning complexity**: Federated generative models undergo distributed updates
  that make version tracking and rollback difficult. If a federated update introduces a
  regression in one site's generative quality, isolating and reverting the problematic
  update is non-trivial.
- **Heterogeneous compute across sites**: Federated generative model training assumes
  comparable compute resources across sites. When sites have vastly different GPU
  configurations, stragglers delay aggregation and create training instability.
- **Prompt consistency across sites**: LLM-based clinical tools produce different outputs
  for semantically equivalent prompts. Ensuring consistent prompt engineering across
  federated sites requires governance protocols that are difficult to enforce.
- **Evaluation across sites**: Evaluating generative output quality (FID for images,
  perplexity for text, statistical fidelity for tabular data) must be performed site-
  specifically because quality depends on local data characteristics, but site-specific
  evaluation requires local labeled validation data.

## 7. Memorization and Privacy

- **Training data memorization**: Large generative models memorize training examples,
  including rare and unique data points. In oncology, a model memorizing a rare case
  presentation may reproduce identifiable patient information in generated outputs — a
  direct HIPAA/GDPR violation.
- **Membership inference attacks**: Adversaries can determine whether a specific patient's
  data was used to train a generative model by querying the model with that patient's data
  and analyzing output likelihood. This privacy attack is particularly effective against
  generative models with high capacity.
- **Extraction attacks on LLMs**: Targeted prompting can extract memorized training data
  from LLMs, including potentially sensitive clinical information used during fine-tuning.

## Summary

Generative AI's limitations in federated oncology are dominated by trust and safety concerns:
hallucination in clinical contexts, the burden of validating unbounded outputs, regulatory
uncertainty, and privacy risks from memorization. These challenges are amplified in federated
settings where consistency, governance, and compute heterogeneity add complexity. Successful
deployment requires defense-in-depth strategies — hallucination mitigation, human-in-the-loop
validation, differential privacy, and robust monitoring — layered on top of the generative
capabilities.
